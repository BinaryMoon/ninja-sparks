## PhotoEditTools

I mentioned in the last issue that I was working on a new tool for it, and it turned out really well. [PhotoEditTools][photoedittools-link] is a website with tools to bulk edit your images/ photos. The newest tool allows you to edit images using a stack of filters. I had the idea for this a couple of years ago and it's the reason I bought the domain, but I hadn't been able to find the time, or the right approach to building it until now.

After buying the domain I built a series of smaller tools for specific bulk editing tasks, resizing images, compressing images, renaming images, and so on. But I wanted to build a single tool that would allow you to create a stack of filters and apply them all at once. I wanted it to be flexible enough to allow you to create your own custom filter stacks, but also have some pre-made stacks for common tasks.

Whilst building it I realised I could also create templates of filter stacks and preload them to create even more specific tools. For example I created a template for creating pixel art, which applies a series of filters to create a pixelated, colour limited, version of your image. I also created a template for turning your photos into retro tv style images, which applies a series of filters to create a retro tv effect.

I was worried that the app would be very complex, but in the end I came up with a simple plugin system that allows me to easily create new filters as individual plugins that can the user can then add and edit.

I'm really happy with how it turned out and enjoy using it myself. I also hope it will be useful to other people who want to bulk edit their images without having to use complex software like Photoshop or GIMP.

All of the tools on the site are free to use, and they run entirely in your browser so it's totally private and secure.

## Project Hail Mary: Behind the Scenes

Project Hail Mary is a science fiction film directed by Andy Weir, based on his novel of the same name. The story follows Ryland Grace, a lone astronaut on a mission to save humanity from an extinction-level threat.

I haven't see it yet, but I have seen the Martian and this looks like it has a similar vibe. What I find fascinating though is that when making the film they used a minimal amount of computer generated imagery. Instead they used practical effects and real sets for as much as they possibly could.

{% include embed-youtube.html video="UxJEmTYlZyk" %}

There's a rock character in the film that plays a prominent role, and it turns out it's a puppet, despite looking like a cg character. They also build full sets of the spaceships and used practical effects for the space scenes. It looks amazing and I'm looking forward to watching it.

{% include embed-youtube.html video="CO6oyFP14rU" %}

These days you get a lot of film makers saying they don't use special effects, but often it turns out they are just minimising the hard work of the huge teams of artists and technicians required to create their movies.

But in this case it seems they really went the extra mile to create as much as they could in the real world. I think this is a great approach. I'm all for using technology to enhance movies, and many times it can make things better, but many films these days rely too much and end up feeling too artificial. I remember watching the Total Recall remake and thinking everything looked so smooth and shiny when compared to the original where every special effect was done in camera.

## Sparked Discoveries

Here’s a handful of links I found intriguing recently:

### [IAMAG Masterclasses](https://iamagtools.com/dashboard?view=masterclasses)

IAMAG is one of those corners of the internet that’s been around for years, quietly doing its thing targetting it's own niche. It grew out of *It’s Art Magazine* and has created a space for artists working in games, film, and animation. The focus is firmly on high-end concept art and visual development, so everything leans toward real industry workflows rather than quick tips or beginner fluff. It’s the sort of place where you can see how professionals actually think, not just what they produce.

What makes it especially interesting right now is their collection of free masterclasses. These are proper, in-depth sessions from working artists, often the same people behind big-name films and games. They are less about step-by-step hand-holding and more about insight, process, and decision-making. If you’re even slightly curious about how top-tier art gets made, they’re well worth a look.

If you like behind the scenes things but don't want full tutorials then you might also like [Befores & Afters](https://beforesandafters.com/). It's specifically about visual effects but has a lot of interesting content about the process of making movies and TV shows.
